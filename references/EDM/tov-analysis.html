memobottle Overview
Section 01

Overall verdict

The brand voice is at its best when writing for product and colour launches. The Butter Yellow and SS Colours series show a team that genuinely understands the voice — playful without being try-hard, confident without overstating. The weaknesses are specific and fixable: em dashes slipping through consistently, some over-written descriptive passages, underuse of key content pillars, and utility emails that feel generic.

memobottle What's Working
Section 02

What's working

Hero headlines are nailing the brief

The colour launch heroes are the strongest copy in the set — short, punchy, playful, and colour-matched in energy. Exactly what the TOV calls for.

"NOW SERVING: BUTTER YELLOW"

"DRINK PINK"

"NUTS ABOUT PISTACHIO"

"'TIS THE SEASON TO BE BUTTERY"

The food metaphor thread running through Butter Yellow — "anything but cookie-cutter", "serving up", "explore the spread", "melts away for good", "our menu of festive favourites" — is the best example of earned dry humour in the entire set. This is the brand at its best.

Colour personality is being matched correctly

The TOV says to match the energy of the colour. This is being executed well across the SS launches:

ColourwayCopy energy
Pink SaltRomantic, wanderer-focused — "the romantics, and the curious"
PistachioModernist, contrast language — "bold, modernist design"
Butter YellowWarmth and lifestyle — "slow mornings and gentle sunshine"
British/Australian English is consistent

"colour", "colourway", "favourite", "flavour" — all correct throughout. No American spellings found in primary campaigns.

B Corp credentials are handled with restraint

"BETTER PRODUCTS / BIGGER PURPOSE" in the footer is exactly right — understated, not self-congratulatory. Consistent across templates.

Product naming is mostly correct

"the memobottle", "the Stainless Steel memobottle" — correctly lowercase and framed throughout the primary campaigns. No instances of "our bottle" in the non-sales emails.

Section 03

Issues & flags

Rule: no em dashes — use a spaced dash ( - ) or rewrite
1. Em dashes — used repeatedly in body copy

This is the most consistent technical violation across the set. It's appearing in the primary (non-sales) emails, which means it's a habit rather than an oversight.

Should be

"making the most of the space you have, minus the bulk"

Found in email

"space you have — making the most of..."

Also appears in subject lines during the sales period: "15% off sitewide continues — limited time only!" and "Free shipping on your Christmas order* — 5 days only".

2. Over-written descriptive passages

Some colour description copy runs longer than it needs to. The TOV benchmark is max 2-3 sentences, often just one. The Pistachio description nails it (one sentence). Pink Salt's "DESIGNED FOR YOU" section drifts:

Tighter version

"For the wanderers, the romantics, and the curious."

Found in email

"Inviting a splash of creative, playful charm into your day, Pink Salt is designed for life in motion, and for those who go with the flow: the wanderers, the romantics, and the curious."

3. Weak superlatives in gifting copy

Butter Yellow EDM 2: "the memobottle is the perfect thoughtful gift for those who appreciate something out-of-the-box." Both "perfect" and "out-of-the-box" are soft clichés. The TOV says confidence is understated — a stronger line would lean into the design angle rather than a gifting trope.

4. Survey and utility emails feel generic

The survey emails ("WE WANT TO HEAR FROM YOU") are clean visually but the copy has no brand personality. A short, dry opener would go a long way. At the moment they read like any DTC brand's survey email.

5. Giveaway copy uses urgency clichés

The Holiday Giveaway EDM uses "LAST CHANCE", "DON'T MISS THIS", and "THE TRIP OF A LIFETIME" — standard urgency phrases that any brand could write. The body is functional but there's no memobottle voice in it.

memobottle Missing Pillars
Section 04

Underused pillars

These are core TOV content pillars that are largely absent from the email set reviewed.

Form factor

The flat/fits-anywhere design angle barely appears. It gets one mention in the Butter Yellow launch (the Tetris-fit line) but isn't being used as a recurring hook in subject lines or opening copy. Given it's the brand's most distinctive differentiator, this is a significant missed opportunity.

"Fits where a bottle shouldn't."

"Pack smarter, not heavier."

"Find space in your bag that you never knew existed."

Lines like these could anchor product launches and new colour EDMs without needing a dedicated section — a single line in the opener is enough.

Sustainability

Beyond the B Corp footer icons, sustainability copy is completely absent from campaign emails. No Water.org mentions, no single-use reduction angle, no matter-of-fact environmental credentials. This is the brand's "why" — and it's not showing up at all in EDMs.

The TOV guideline is clear: sustainability is matter-of-fact, never preachy. One line is often enough.

"Carry water without creating waste."

"One less excuse to reach for a single-use bottle."

The understated flex

The TOV describes a structure called "the understated flex" — confident, minimal lines that let the product speak. This style is absent from the emails reviewed, with copy defaulting to longer descriptive passages when a single line would land harder.

"Different by design."

"Less is more."

"Multi-award winning design. You'll see why."
